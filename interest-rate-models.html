Interest-rate modelling

Purpose & modelling landscape

Interest rate models describe the evolution of short-term rates, the entire yield curve, or discrete market rates. They price swaps and swaptions, support balance-sheet asset-liability management, and value exotic derivatives whose cash flows depend on future term structures.

Modelling philosophies. Short-rate dynamics (e.g., Vasicek, CIR) track a single instantaneous rate; forward-rate Heath–Jarrow–Morton (HJM) models evolve the entire forward curve; market models such as the Libor Market Model (LMM) evolve observable forward rates. Each balances tractability, realism, and calibration effort.

Risk-free & neutral rates

The risk-free rate is a proxy derived from collateralised overnight funding, overnight index swaps (OIS), or high-quality government securities. It anchors the discount curve for collateralised cash flows under modern XVA-aware pricing.[1]

The neutral (long-run) rate represents the equilibrium level consistent with stable inflation and full employment. In mean-reverting short-rate models, it is the long-run mean \( \theta \).

Calibration aligns model parameters to today’s yield curve, while the neutral rate guides macro stress scenarios and long-horizon projections.

General frameworks

Short-rate models

Model the instantaneous rate \( r_t \) via stochastic differential equations (SDEs). The canonical mean-reverting form is

$$ dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t $$

Vasicek and Hull–White deliver Gaussian rates (negative values allowed), while CIR uses a square-root diffusion to enforce non-negativity.

Forward-rate (HJM) models

Heath–Jarrow–Morton specifies dynamics for the instantaneous forward curve \( f(t,T) \). Drift restrictions ensure no arbitrage. HJM is flexible but usually requires factor reduction or discretisation for implementation.

Market models

Evolve observable rates such as forward Libor or swap rates. Calibration matches quoted caplet/swaption volatility surfaces: the Libor Market Model (LMM/BGM) is a popular choice for callable exotics via Monte Carlo.

Affine & multi-curve extensions

Affine term-structure models yield exponential-affine bond prices with closed-form yields. Post-crisis frameworks separate discount and projection curves, embedding credit/funding valuation adjustments (XVAs).

Representative models & formulas

Vasicek (Ornstein–Uhlenbeck)

$$ dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t $$

Gaussian, mean-reverting; supports analytic bond prices but permits negative rates.

Pros: closed-form solutions. Cons: negative-rate risk.

Cox–Ingersoll–Ross (CIR)

$$ dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\sqrt{r_t}\,dW_t $$

Square-root diffusion keeps rates non-negative; bond prices admit closed-form expressions.

Pros: positivity. Cons: calibration to vol surfaces harder.

Hull–White (extended Vasicek)

$$ dr_t = \kappa(\theta(t) - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t $$

Time-dependent drift fits the initial curve exactly and retains analytic swaption formulas.

Pros: flexible fit. Cons: negative rates possible.

Libor Market Model

Evolves forward Libor rates under their natural measures, matching caplet volatilities; supports callable exotics via Monte Carlo simulation.

Pros: market-consistent. Cons: Monte Carlo intensive.

Model selection checklist

Consideration Short-rate models Market models
Calibration inputs Yield curves, swaption/swap spreads, mean-reversion assumptions. Caplet/swaption vol surfaces, forward-rate observations.
Analytical tractability Closed-form bonds; analytic swaption formulas (Vasicek, Hull–White). Monte Carlo heavy; limited closed-form payoffs.
Term-structure twists One-factor models struggle; multi-factor extensions handle twists. Rich factor structure captures tenor-dependent volatility.
Negative rates Gaussian models allow; CIR avoids but may be restrictive. Depends on modelling choice (shifted lognormal etc.).
Use cases Balance-sheet ALM, risk-neutral discounting, stress testing. Exotic derivatives, callable products, structured notes.
