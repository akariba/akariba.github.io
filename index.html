Diffusion foundations

Brownian motion

Louis Bachelier first applied Brownian motion to asset prices in 1900, modelling short-term price changes as independent and normally distributed.[1]

A standard Brownian motion \( (W_t)_{t \ge 0} \) (Wiener process) starts at zero, has continuous paths, and features independent, normally distributed increments. It is both a martingale and a Markov process, making it an ideal driver of random shocks.[1] Geometric Brownian motion (GBM) exponentiates Brownian motion to keep simulated prices positive.

Diffusion equations. $$ dW_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, dt), \qquad dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t $$ Analytical moments follow \( \mathbb{E}[S_T] = S_0 e^{\mu T} \) with log-normal terminal distributions.[1]

Key properties

  • Continuous paths with normally distributed increments ensure tractable analytics.[1]
  • Independent increments deliver the Markov property—future moves depend only on today’s state.[1]
  • Martingale behaviour under the pricing measure enables arbitrage-free valuation.

How GBM powers pricing

  • Equity & FX options. Black–Scholes assumes GBM to derive closed-form call and put prices.
  • Monte Carlo valuation. GBM drives path-dependent products such as Asian, barrier, or lookback options.
  • Risk factor generation. VaR and stress simulations use GBM to project equity or FX moves under baseline assumptions.

Modeling considerations

  • Drift \( \mu \) encodes the long-run growth rate; under risk-neutral pricing it is replaced by the risk-free rate.
  • Volatility \( \sigma \) controls dispersion and option time value; calibration anchors it to implied or historical vol.
  • Time discretisation (steps per year) balances realism and computational cost when simulating many paths.

No-arbitrage pricing

Martingales

A martingale keeps its conditional expectation equal to its current value—the best forecast of tomorrow is today. In arbitrage-free markets the discounted asset price \( e^{-rt} S_t \) is a martingale under the risk-neutral measure, which underpins consistent pricing across derivatives.[2]

No-arbitrage guarantee. If discounted prices are martingales, any zero-cost, self-financing strategy has zero expected profit, locking in no-arbitrage pricing across products.[2]

Pricing implications

  • Risk-neutral valuation. Derivative values equal discounted expected payoffs under \( \mathbb{Q} \).
  • Hedging consistency. Replication arguments hinge on discounted hedged portfolios forming martingales.
  • Calibrated curves. Forward rates, discount factors, and drifts align so that tradable assets stay arbitrage-free.

Risk management usage

  • Validates P&L explain: hedged books should exhibit martingale-like behaviour under the pricing measure.
  • Supports exposure simulation (EE/EPE) by discounting future values to today under \( \mathbb{Q} \).
  • Provides a framework to stress break-even drifts when testing hedging robustness.

Beyond GBM

Other stochastic processes

Real markets exhibit jumps, clustering, and regime shifts. Extending beyond GBM enables richer calibration to observed data and more realistic stress tests.

Poisson & jump processes

Poisson arrivals layered on diffusion capture rare events such as defaults, rating downgrades, or flash crashes. They enrich models with fat tails and gap risk.

  • Pricing: jump-to-default adjustments for credit derivatives and barrier options.
  • Risk: scenario generation for sudden spread widening and liquidity shocks.

Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (mean reversion)

Mean-reverting dynamics \( dX_t = \kappa(\theta - X_t)dt + \sigma dW_t \) model factors pulled toward a long-run level.

  • Rates: calibrate short-rate models (Vasicek, Hull–White) for yield-curve evolution.
  • Commodities: capture inventory effects and seasonal pulls in energy and metals markets.

Hidden Markov models

Hidden Markov models infer latent market regimes, switching between high- and low-volatility states to reflect macro shifts.

  • Trading: regime-aware allocation, volatility targeting, and signal filtering.
  • Risk oversight: early warning dashboards and stress library triggers.
Choose the process that mirrors your economic story: GBM for daily diffusion, jump processes for shocks, mean reversion for anchored factors, and hidden Markov models for regime detection.
